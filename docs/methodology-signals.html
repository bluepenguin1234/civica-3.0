How Civica Signals Works

Last updated: June 2026

Important: Signals is derived from public municipal records and may contain extraction errors or lag the source documents. It is not legal, investment, or bidding advice. Every item links to the official source document — verify against it before acting.

What it reads

Civica Signals monitors the public meeting documents that Massachusetts towns publish on their own websites — the agendas and minutes of the four boards where development decisions actually happen: the Planning Board, Zoning Board of Appeals, Select Board / City Council, and Conservation Commission. No private feeds, no scraped listings — the same PDFs any resident can download.

How events are extracted

  1. Collection. New agenda and minutes PDFs are fetched from each town's website on a weekly cadence and archived. Every document's source URL is kept.
  2. Extraction. An AI model reads each document and pulls out qualifying items — development projects, zoning changes, 40B filings, subdivisions, infrastructure work, municipal property moves — with the applicant, representatives, scale, and stage only as stated in the document. Numbers and names are never inferred; if the document doesn't say, the field stays empty.
  3. Verification. Each extracted event carries a confidence score and is checked against the source text before publication. Low-confidence items are held for human review. Routine administrative business (minutes approvals, licenses, appointments) is excluded.
  4. Story linking. The same project appears across many meetings over months. Events at the same address (or matching project names) are grouped into one project story with a stage timeline — proposed → hearing → approved.

Update cadence — and an honest note on latency

Documents are collected weekly. Signals is weekly monitoring of public records, not a real-time feed: agendas appear days before a meeting (your earliest signal), while minutes are often posted weeks after the meeting they describe. The date shown on every event is the meeting date, not the date we found it.

Known limitations

  • Extraction can be wrong. Stages, types, and summaries are machine-read from messy documents. The source link is on every item for a reason.
  • Scanned documents are deferred. Some towns post image-only PDFs (no machine-readable text). Those are archived but not yet extracted — for some towns that means agendas are thin while minutes are rich, or vice versa.
  • Coverage follows the registry. A town's data is only as fresh as its website. Per-town freshness is shown in the dashboard's coverage strip, and stale towns are flagged in our own validation before every publish.
  • Public documents only. Pre-application conversations, private deals, and anything not yet on a posted agenda are invisible to Signals by design.

What Signals is not

Signals never feeds into Civica's town scores — the scores remain 100% federal data and cannot be influenced by development activity, advertisers, or anyone else. Signals is a separate research layer for professionals who need to know what's moving through town halls.
