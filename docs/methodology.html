Methodology

The score in 30 seconds

Every town gets one number from 0 to 100, built entirely from federal government data.

1

Measure

For every town we pull hard federal numbers — rent, wages, jobs, crime, school test results, population growth, and more. All public, all replicable.

2

Rank

Each number becomes a 0–100 ranking — how this town compares to all 12,192 others. Where lower is better (crime, rent burden), we flip it so higher always means better.

3

Add it up

The rankings roll into five dimensions, each capped so none can take over. Add the five and you get the Civica Score.

Town data
About 58% of every score is resolved at the town level (crime, income, growth, town size, schools). The other ~42% is inherited from the town’s parent county (wages, home-price appreciation, climate risk, migration, permits) — because those genuinely operate at a regional scale, and faking them per-town would be dishonest. Every town report shows the split.

Each metric is scored as a national percentile, so a town is always compared to all 12,192 others. T = resolved at the town level; C = inherited from the parent county. The percentage is the metric’s share of its dimension.

🏠 Affordability — 25 points

Rent burden · 60% T — HUD 2-bedroom Fair Market Rent × 12 ÷ ZIP-allocated town income. Lower is better. The core question: does local income cover local rent?

Appreciation quality · 40% C — FHFA 3-year average home-price change. A healthy 3–7% pace scores best; too hot or falling is penalized. (No home-value level in the model — that left with Zillow.)

💼 Economy — 24 points

Wage level · 35% C — BLS average annual wage. Higher wages, stronger labor market.

Sector quality · 25% C — BLS employment weighted by industry. Professional and finance score higher; retail and legacy manufacturing lower.

Job diversity · 20% C — how spread-out employment is across industries (HHI). More diversified = more resilient.

Town income growth · 20% T — change in IRS ZIP-allocated town income between two filing years.

🛡️ Safety & Place — 22 points

Violent crime / 100k · 30% T — FBI NIBRS, reporting agencies matched to places by name. Towns with no matched agency inherit the county/peer-tier rate — flagged, never penalized.

Property crime / 100k · 20% T — FBI NIBRS burglary, larceny, vehicle theft, arson.

Town scale · 20% T — population percentile, a proxy for how built-up and serviced a place is.

Amenity density · 15% C — Census County Business Patterns establishments per 1,000 residents.

Physical risk · 15% C — FEMA flood (40%) + NOAA storm (35%) + USFS wildfire (25%), inverted so lower risk scores higher.

📈 Growth — 15 points

5-year population growth · 35% T — Census sub-county estimates, 2020→2025.

Growth vs. county + momentum · 25% T — the town’s growth above or below its county, plus its most recent 1-year trend — its pure growth “alpha.”

Net migration · 20% C — county net moves per 1,000 residents (Census).

In-mover income quality · 10% C — IRS income of people moving in vs. moving out.

Permit pipeline · 10% C — Census new housing units permitted.

🎓 Schools — 14 points

Proficiency · 100% T — public-school math + reading proficiency from the US Dept. of Education’s EDFacts (school year 2017–18). State tests aren’t comparable across states, so each school is ranked within its own state, then enrollment-weighted to the town by school ZIP code. About 4 in 5 towns get their own school data; the rest inherit a county/peer-tier estimate (flagged). Capped at 14 points so a strong-schools town can’t run away with the score.

A few signals are built by combining two or more federal sources and pushing them below the county line. None of these appear on consumer real-estate sites.

Town income (ZIP-allocated)

How: IRS ZIP-code total income ÷ returns, allocated to each town through the Census ZCTA→place file, weighted by land-area overlap.

Why: No federal source publishes a survey-grade town median income. This address-share estimate varies town-to-town for real — it’s how Civica resolves income below the county line. It’s labeled an approximation everywhere it appears, and falls back to county income for the ~1% of towns with no ZIP match.

Rent burden

How: HUD 2-bedroom Fair Market Rent × 12 ÷ ZIP-allocated town income.

Why: This is the core affordability signal. Under 25% means incomes comfortably carry rents; 25–35% is stretched; over 35% means affordability is a real constraint — regardless of list prices.

Sector quality

How: employment share × an industry quality weight, summed across BLS supersectors (professional/finance 1.30×, healthcare/education 1.00×, retail and legacy manufacturing 0.60×).

Why: Two places can have the same number of jobs but very different economic quality. A town built on professional services is structurally more resilient than one built on retail.

Physical-risk index

How: FEMA flood claims per person (40%) + NOAA storm damage per person (35%) + USFS wildfire rank (25%), all flipped so lower risk scores higher.

Why: Insurance costs are climbing fastest in high-risk areas. This uses actual realized losses — the same hazard data FEMA uses for its National Risk Index — not modeled guesses.

School proficiency is EDFacts SY2017–18 (the latest complete year available) and is ranked within state because state tests aren’t comparable nationally. ~42% of each score is county-inherited. Civica covers self-governing US towns and cities with at least 1,000 residents — Census incorporated places plus governing towns (Minor Civil Divisions) — New England towns and standalone townships elsewhere that are at least 70% unincorporated (e.g. New York towns like Brookhaven) — about 12,192 nationwide. Unincorporated communities (Census Designated Places), townships that mostly wrap an incorporated place we already list, and sub-1,000-pop places aren’t included. Finally, the 58% “town-resolved” figure is a structural maximum: rent burden uses HUD Fair Market Rents, which are set at the metro/county level (only the income side varies by town), and where a town’s schools or crime fall back to a county estimate, those points are effectively county-inherited even though the model counts them toward the town total.

What counts as a “town,” and why 12,192?

A town here is a self-governing municipality with at least 1,000 residents — a US Census incorporated place, or a governing town/township (a Minor Civil Division) — in New England or a state like New York. There are 12,192 nationwide. Below 1,000 people, town income, crime, and growth get too sparse to score honestly. Unincorporated communities (Census Designated Places) and townships that mostly overlap an incorporated place we already list are excluded — the latter to avoid double-counting.

Why is ~42% of the score inherited from the county?

Some things genuinely operate at a regional scale — average wages, home-price appreciation, climate risk, migration, and permits are county-or-metro phenomena, not town ones. Faking them per-town would be dishonest. So Civica resolves what it credibly can at the town level (crime, income, growth, scale, schools ≈ 58%) and clearly labels the rest as county-inherited. Every town report shows the split.

How accurate is town-level income and crime?

Both are real federal data, approximately placed. Town income is IRS ZIP-code income allocated to places by land-area overlap — it varies town-to-town for real, but it’s an address-share estimate, not a survey median. Crime is FBI NIBRS matched from each reporting agency to a place; county sheriffs (who cover unincorporated land) stay in the county pool, and towns with no matched agency inherit the county/peer-tier rate — flagged, never penalized for not reporting.

Why is there no home price or “price-to-rent”?

Dropping Zillow — the only non-federal source — removed the home-value level. Rather than fake it, affordability is rebuilt on what federal data supports at the town level: rent burden (HUD rent vs. town income) plus FHFA appreciation quality. The result: Civica is now 100% federal data.

Why are schools only 14 points?

Schools matter, but this isn’t a school-rating site — it’s a whole-town read. Capping schools at 14 keeps strong-schools suburbs from dominating the score while still rewarding them. And because state tests aren’t comparable across states, school scores are ranked within each state, not nationally.

How often are scores updated?

Scores are recalculated as new federal data releases. Current scores reflect FBI NIBRS 2024, IRS income 2021–2022, Census sub-county estimates 2020–2025, HUD rents FY2026, and EDFacts SY2017–18.

Is this financial advice?

No. Civica scores are for information and research only — not financial, investment, or real-estate advice. Real-estate decisions involve local factors and personal circumstances no model fully captures. Always consult a qualified professional before buying.
