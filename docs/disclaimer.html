What Civica Scores Measure

Civica scores are composite indices derived entirely from federal government data, normalized relative to all 12,192 scored US towns (incorporated places plus governing towns/townships — New England and standalone ones elsewhere, with at least 1,000 residents). A high score means a town ranks well on five dimensions — affordability, economy, safety & place, growth, and schools — relative to all other scored towns. About 58% of each score is resolved at the town level (crime, income, growth, scale, schools) and the remainder is inherited from the town's parent county. A high score does not mean the town is a good investment for your specific situation, timeline, or risk tolerance.

What Civica Scores Do Not Measure

  • Specific neighborhoods, ZIP codes, or individual properties within a town.
  • School quality (not included in current model — see methodology).
  • Future economic shocks, employer exits, natural disasters, or policy changes.
  • Your personal financial situation, debt-to-income ratio, or credit standing.
  • Local zoning rules, HOA restrictions, or title encumbrances.
  • Current mortgage rates available to you specifically.

Data Currency

Scores are based on data available as of the scoring engine's last run (Q2 2026). Key data lags:

  • FHFA HPI: through Q4 2024
  • BLS QCEW wages: 2023 annual
  • BEA per capita income: 2023
  • IRS SOI migration: 2022–23 tax year
  • FBI NIBRS crime: 2024 national master file
  • FEMA NFIP: 10-year average through 2023
  • Census population: July 2023 estimate

Town vs. County Coverage

Roughly 58% of each town's score is resolved at the town level (crime, income, growth, population scale, schools). The remaining ~42% is inherited from the town's parent county (wages, appreciation, climate risk, migration, permits) because those metrics genuinely operate at a regional scale. Civica discloses this split on every town report and does not claim town-level precision for county-inherited metrics. Town income is a ZIP→place approximation, and town crime is mapped from reporting agencies to places — both are labeled as approximations.

Strong Buy / Buy / Hold / Caution Labels

These are Civica's shorthand for score tiers (Strong Buy ≥ 62/100, Buy ≥ 52, Hold ≥ 44, Caution < 44), not personalized investment recommendations. A town labeled Caution may be an excellent choice for some buyers; a town labeled Strong Buy may be wrong for others. The label reflects relative fundamentals, not an instruction to transact.

Source Data Licenses

All Civica data comes from US federal government sources (FBI, IRS, Census, BLS, BEA, FHFA, FEMA, NOAA, USFS, USDA, HUD), which are in the public domain. There are no commercial or proprietary data sources in the model. All source data attribution is provided on each town report page.
